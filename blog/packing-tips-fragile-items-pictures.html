Packing Tips for Fragile Items and Pictures

2026-05-18 · 9 min read · Packing · technique

We run our own staff training centre — one of only a handful of UK removal companies that does. Among the most-taught modules is packing fragile items. The reason: when self-packed boxes arrive damaged, the cause is almost always one of five technique errors, and all five are avoidable with proper materials and ten minutes of explanation. This article is the same explanation our packers learn on day one. If you’re self-packing the fragile items in your home, read it before you start.

Get the materials right first

Cheap materials are responsible for more damage than any other single factor. Before you pack a single plate:

  • Triple-wall cartons for fragile. Not single-wall “moving boxes” from the supermarket. Triple-wall stays rigid under stack pressure. Available from our packing materials page.
  • Acid-free tissue paper for direct contact with china and silver. Newspaper transfers ink onto porcelain glaze and silver tarnishes faster against newsprint.
  • 50mm vinyl packing tape. Cheap parcel tape releases in a warm lorry. Get the proper stuff.
  • Anti-static bubble wrap for electronics. Standard bubble can carry a static charge that damages circuit boards.
  • Dividered carton inserts for glassware. The cardboard egg-crate dividers stop glasses banging into each other and concentrate force away from the rim.

China and porcelain — the right technique

The single biggest mistake people make: stacking plates flat. Plates lying flat break. Plates standing on edge survive.

  • Wrap each plate individually in two sheets of acid-free tissue. Tape closed.
  • Stand them vertically in the carton — like books on a shelf. Pad the bottom of the carton with crumpled tissue first.
  • Fill all gaps with more tissue or with smaller items wrapped separately. The plates must not move when the carton is shaken gently.
  • Cups go separately — handles broken off cups are the second most common fragile damage. Wrap each cup with tissue stuffed inside the bowl (so the handle has support), then wrap the outside.
  • Saucers stack horizontally with tissue between each one, in their own column inside the carton.

Glassware and stemware

The dividered carton insert is the secret. Without it, glasses bang together and one of them dies.

  • Stuff tissue inside the bowl of each glass. The most common stemware failure is the bowl breaking inward — a tissue plug stops this.
  • Wrap the outside in bubble or tissue. Place stem-up in the dividered carton.
  • Heavier glasses (tumblers, brandy snifters) go on the bottom row. Lighter wine and champagne flutes go above.
  • Top with a padded layer — a folded tea-towel or a sheet of bubble before sealing.
  • Mark the carton FRAGILE on all six sides. Lorry crews don’t always see the top sticker.

Framed pictures and mirrors

Glass under tension breaks easily. Three steps:

  • Tape an X across the front of the glass with low-tack painter’s tape. If the glass breaks in transit, the tape holds the pieces together and prevents them slashing the frame and the canvas behind.
  • Wrap the whole frame in tissue first, then bubble. Tissue protects gilt and lacquer; bubble absorbs impact.
  • Use telescopic mirror cartons for anything over 60cm. These adjust to any size and have corner padding built in. Plain corrugated wrap is fine for smaller pieces.

For genuine art — original oils, watercolours, antique prints — we strongly recommend either using our full packing service or our custom timber crates for higher-value items.

Electronics — the anti-static rule

Modern TVs, computers and audio equipment are sensitive to two things in transit: shock and static electricity.

  • Use original boxes where you have them. They’re shaped to absorb shock better than any aftermarket carton.
  • Where you don’t, use anti-static bubble wrap. Wrap the unit completely and pack into a triple-wall carton.
  • Photograph the cable arrangement before disconnecting. Then label each cable with the device and the port it came from. Bag the cables with the device.
  • Remote controls in a labelled ziplock bag, taped to the inside of the device’s carton.
  • Hard drives travel with you — never in the lorry. Same for laptops if you can carry them.

Lamps, ornaments and ceramics

  • Lamps: shade separated from base. Shades wrapped in tissue and packed loose (not crushed). Bases wrapped in bubble.
  • Bulbs out and packed in their original sleeves or a labelled bag.
  • Ornaments — individually tissue-wrapped first, then bubble. Standing them on a layer of crumpled tissue in the carton.
  • Ceramic figurines (Lladró, Royal Doulton): tissue between every limb and accessory. These are particularly vulnerable at the joints.
  • Vases — fill the inside with crushed tissue before wrapping. Empty vases collapse under sideways pressure.

The five most common self-packing mistakes

If you do nothing else, avoid these five — they account for about 80% of self-pack damage we see at the other end:

  • Boxes too heavy. Books and files in a large carton bust the base. Books go in small book cartons (max 25cm cubed).
  • Cheap tape. Pound-shop parcel tape releases in a warm lorry. Always use 50mm vinyl.
  • Plates stacked flat. Vertical or nothing.
  • Wine glasses without internal padding. Tissue inside the bowl is the rule.
  • Furniture not wrapped before leaving the room. Use our packing service or borrow blankets — wrap before the doorway, not in the lorry.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use newspaper instead of tissue paper?

For glassware yes (with internal tissue still in the bowl). For china and silver no — newsprint transfers ink. Tissue is cheap; buy proper packing tissue.

How do I label fragile cartons?

Mark FRAGILE on all six sides of the carton, not just the top. Lorry crews stack from above and don’t always see the top sticker once the box is loaded.

What about my TV?

Original box if you have it. If not, anti-static bubble wrap then triple-wall carton with corner-block protection. Carry the TV upright at all times — don’t lay it flat.

Do you offer just fragile-only packing?
