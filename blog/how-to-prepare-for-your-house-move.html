How to Prepare for Your House Move — A Real 8-Week Plan

2026-05-18 · 10 min read · Practical · checklists

Moving home is consistently ranked among the most stressful life events. After 40 years of moving Sussex families we can tell you that almost all of the stress comes from the fifty small decisions that pile up in the last fortnight — not from the actual move day itself. The single biggest predictor of a calm move is doing the right things at the right time in the eight weeks beforehand. This is the same week-by-week plan we give every customer who books a survey, presented for anyone who’s about to move.

Eight weeks out — confirm the big decisions

This is the “big decisions” week. Three things to handle now:

  • Book your remover. Better movers fill up 6–10 weeks ahead, especially for end-of-month dates. We always recommend a survey first — it’ll surface things (a piano, a pond, a cat that lives in the loft) that affect the quote.
  • Confirm your solicitor. If you haven’t already, this is the week. Get a fixed-fee quote in writing.
  • Start a moving file. One folder (paper or digital — but ideally both) where every email, every quote, every list goes. By move week you’ll have 80+ items in it.

Also: tell the school. If you’re moving out of catchment, give the new school time. Most Sussex primaries want at least a half-term’s notice; many secondaries want more.

Six weeks out — declutter and inventory

Six weeks out is the optimum decluttering window. Far enough that you can do it without panic; close enough that you remember to. The rule: anything you haven’t used in 18 months is a candidate for sale, donation or recycling.

  • One room per weekend. Bedroom, then spare room, then dining room, then kitchen, then loft. Don’t try to do the whole house in one go — you’ll burn out.
  • Sort into four piles: keep, sell, donate, recycle. The “sell” pile usually shrinks once people realise the time it takes — Facebook Marketplace is fine for big-ticket items; for smaller items, donation is usually quicker.
  • Inventory the loft. Lofts are where surprises live. Get up there now. Decide what’s coming, what’s going to the tip, what’s being donated.
  • Cancel or transfer subscriptions. Gym, magazine, gardening service, box deliveries. Some can be paused; some can be moved to the new address.

Four weeks out — booking and logistics

  • Confirm the move date in writing. Especially if it’s contingent on a chain. We’re used to chains and don’t bill if a date slides.
  • Order packing materials. If self-packing, use our materials page to work out volume. Better to have spares than run out at midnight.
  • Empty the freezer. Stop refilling it. Move-day defrost is annoying.
  • Disconnect specialist installs. Satellite dishes, smart-home hubs, alarms, wall-mounted TVs — book the engineers now.
  • Apply for parking suspensions at both old and new addresses if either is in a permit zone. Sussex councils need 5–10 working days’ notice. We arrange this for you if you’ve booked us.

Two weeks out — start packing

  • Pack rooms you don’t use daily. Spare bedroom, dining room, garden room, garage. Don’t touch the kitchen or main bedroom yet.
  • Take meter readings. Gas, electric and water at both addresses on the day of completion. Photograph with the date stamp on.
  • Inform pet boarders. If your dog or cat is staying with someone on move day, confirm dates now. We strongly recommend pets not be onsite during the move.
  • Cash in vouchers. Local-business gift cards, restaurant credit, leisure-centre memberships. Either spend them or transfer them.
  • Start the address-change list. Council, doctor, dentist, optician, vet, gym, bank, building society, employer, HMRC, DVLA, pension, insurance, TV licence, energy, water, broadband, mobile, streaming. The Royal Mail redirect (£33.99 for 3 months) covers the slow ones.

The week of the move

  • Pack a “first night” box. Kettle, mugs, tea, coffee, sugar, milk, bedding for one bed, towels, toiletries, phone chargers, painkillers, snacks, basic cutlery and plates for one meal. Carry this in your own car. It is the single most useful thing you can do.
  • Defrost the fridge and freezer. 24 hours before move day. Put a towel in the bottom to soak meltwater.
  • Photograph cable arrangements. TV, internet, electronics — un-cabling is much easier when you know what went where.
  • Charge everything. Phones, laptops, power banks. You will not find your charger for at least a day.
  • Bag the essentials separately. Passports, birth certificates, the dog’s vaccination card, the conveyancer’s contact details. These travel with you, not in the lorry.

Move day — what to expect

  • Be there for 8am. Crew typically arrives 8–8:30. Coffee for the team is appreciated but not required.
  • Walk through with the crew leader. Confirm what’s moving and what’s staying. Point out anything especially fragile.
  • Final meter readings. Before the new owners’ clock starts.
  • Leave a note for the new owners. Where the meter cupboard is, when the bin day is, what the broadband password might be. Small kindnesses go a long way.
  • Don’t supervise the loading. Let the crew do their job. Your role is to be available for questions, not to direct the load. Your real job is handling the destination property when the lorry arrives.

Week one in the new home

  • Find the stopcock and consumer unit. Before you need them. Saves a panicked 2am search.
  • Test smoke and CO alarms. Replace any battery that beeps.
  • Bleed the radiators if the central heating’s been off. Particularly relevant for older Sussex properties.
  • Register with the GP and dentist. Appointments can run 4–8 weeks out — book in week one rather than week four.
  • Sign up for council services. Bin collection, recycling rota, parking permits, garden waste. Each Sussex council has different rules.
  • Walk the neighbourhood. Find the nearest shop, post box, bus stop and pub. Fastest way to feel at home.

The mistakes that derail an otherwise good plan

An eight-week plan only works if it survives contact with reality. We’ve seen the same six mistakes scuttle an otherwise organised move — some of them obvious in hindsight, some of them counter-intuitive enough to catch even experienced movers.

Booking the remover before the survey. Phone quotes for anything beyond a single bedroom are guesswork. A 30–45 minute in-home survey lets the remover see access, count cartons properly, and price accurately. Customers who book a remover off a phone quote almost always pay more on move day, in surcharges and overtime, than they would have on a fixed quote after a survey.

Packing the kitchen and bathroom too early. These are the rooms you genuinely use until the morning of the move. Packing them three weeks out means living out of cardboard for a fortnight, eating takeaways every night, and unpacking again to find a single saucepan. Leave these for last and pack a single ‘first night’ carton clearly labelled.

Underestimating the loft, garage and shed. Almost every survey we do finds the loft contains roughly twice what the customer thought. A weekend of pulling everything down and laying it out on the floor before survey day means the quote reflects reality, not optimism. It’s also the cheapest opportunity to declutter you’ll have all year.

Leaving the change-of-address admin too late. DVLA, GP, dentist, banks, insurers, employer payroll, HMRC, electoral roll. Each is ten minutes online. Doing them all in the week of the move means doing them badly — or forgetting one (it’s almost always the insurer, who then declines a claim two months later because the address wasn’t updated).

Failing to plan the chain’s communication. On the day of completion, four sets of solicitors and three estate agents are trying to release funds and confirm keys. Set up a single WhatsApp group with your conveyancer, agent and remover. One message per hour beats fifteen phone calls from different parties asking the same question. Your remover should be in that loop too — they’re sitting in a lorry waiting for the green light.

Not letting the crew lead on move day. The single biggest day-of-move mistake is the customer trying to direct the loading sequence. The crew knows what goes in first, what needs to be accessible at the other end, and what should never travel touching what. Make tea, point at rooms, answer questions — but trust the crew on the load order. Move day goes 20% faster when the customer stays out of the lorry.

Frequently asked questions

How early should I book a remover?

For end-of-month dates in May–September, 6–10 weeks ahead. Mid-week mid-month dates can sometimes be booked 2–3 weeks ahead. The earlier you book, the more choice of slot.

What if my move date slides because of the chain?

Tell us as soon as you know. We don’t charge for date changes if you give us reasonable notice. See our terms.

Do I have to be there on move day?

Yes for at least the start of the load and the start of the unload. If you can’t, give us a written authority and a key arrangement — common for executor moves.

What should I do with hazardous items?

Don’t pack petrol, paint, gas bottles, ammunition, fireworks or pressurised canisters in the boxes. We can’t legally transport them. Take them yourself or dispose of them at the tip.

Will the crew take the cardboard away after?

If you’ve booked the unpacking service, yes — same day. Otherwise we’ll come back later in the week (free of charge if we’re within range) to collect empty boxes.
